# High School US History Guide
**Course Level:** Typically Grade 11 (US History), Grade 11 (AP US History)
**Prerequisites:** World History or Geography (for regular); strong reading/writing (for AP)
**Applies To:** All high school US History and AP US History courses

---

## Overview

US History examines the political, economic, social, and cultural development of the United States from pre-Columbian times to present. Students analyze primary sources, interpret historical events, and understand continuity and change over time.

**College/Career Readiness:** US History is required for high school graduation in most states and builds critical thinking, analysis, and evidence-based argumentation essential for college and careers in law, government, journalism, education, and business.

**State Requirements:** US History is typically required for graduation. Many states require state assessments or EOC exams.

**AP US History (APUSH):** College-level course emphasizing historical thinking skills, thematic analysis, and document-based argumentation.

---

## Key Periods and Themes

### Periods (AP US History Framework)

1. **Pre-Columbian to 1607:** Native American societies, European exploration
2. **1607-1754:** Colonial societies, regional differences
3. **1754-1800:** Revolution, Constitution, early republic
4. **1800-1848:** Jeffersonian democracy, expansion, reform movements
5. **1844-1877:** Manifest Destiny, Civil War, Reconstruction
6. **1865-1898:** Industrial America, immigration, Gilded Age
7. **1890-1945:** Progressivism, imperialism, WWI, Great Depression, WWII
8. **1945-1980:** Cold War, civil rights, Vietnam, social movements
9. **1980-Present:** Conservative resurgence, globalization, post-9/11 America

---

### Themes (AP US History)

1. **American and National Identity (NAT):** Ideas about American identity, citizenship, democracy
2. **Politics and Power (POL):** Political institutions, parties, debates over government role
3. **Work, Exchange, and Technology (WXT):** Economic development, labor, technology
4. **Culture and Society (CUL):** Religious, philosophical, artistic, cultural developments
5. **Migration and Settlement (MIG):** Immigration, internal migration, demographic changes
6. **Geography and Environment (GEO):** Human-environment interaction, expansion
7. **America in the World (WOR):** Diplomacy, foreign policy, wars, global role

---

## Historical Thinking Skills (AP US History)

**Skill 1: Developments and Processes**
- Identify and explain historical developments and processes

**Skill 2: Sourcing and Situation**
- Analyze sourcing and situation of primary sources

**Skill 3: Claims and Evidence**
- Make and support historical claims with evidence

**Skill 4: Contextualization**
- Situate events in broader historical context

**Skill 5: Making Connections**
- Compare, explain causation, analyze continuity and change (CCOT)

**Skill 6: Argumentation**
- Develop and support historical argument

## Major Topics and Events

**Colonial Period:** Jamestown, Plymouth, 13 colonies, triangular trade, Great Awakening
**Revolution:** French and Indian War, taxation, Declaration of Independence, Revolutionary War
**Constitution:** Articles of Confederation, Constitutional Convention, Federalist vs. Anti-Federalist, Bill of Rights
**Early Republic:** Washington, Jefferson, War of 1812, Era of Good Feelings
**Expansion:** Louisiana Purchase, Manifest Destiny, Mexican-American War, Indian Removal
**Antebellum:** Market Revolution, Second Great Awakening, reform movements, abolitionism
**Civil War:** Causes (slavery, sectionalism), war, Emancipation Proclamation
**Reconstruction:** 13th/14th/15th Amendments, Freedmen's Bureau, end of Reconstruction
**Gilded Age:** Industrialization, immigration, urbanization, labor movements, political machines
**Progressivism:** Muckrakers, social/political reforms, suffrage, TR/Wilson presidencies
**Imperialism:** Spanish-American War, Philippines, Open Door Policy
**WWI:** Causes, US entry, homefront, Treaty of Versailles
**1920s:** Roaring Twenties, consumer culture, Red Scare, immigration restriction
**Great Depression:** Causes, Hoover's response, FDR's New Deal
**WWII:** Causes, US entry, homefront (internment, women/African Americans in workforce), Holocaust
**Cold War:** Containment, Truman Doctrine, Korean War, McCarthyism, arms race, Cuban Missile Crisis, Vietnam War
**Civil Rights:** Brown v. Board, Montgomery Bus Boycott, MLK, Civil Rights Acts (1964, 1965), Black Power
**1960s-70s:** JFK/LBJ, Great Society, counterculture, women's movement, Watergate
**Reagan Era:** Conservatism, Reagan Revolution, end of Cold War
**Modern America:** Clinton, 9/11, War on Terror, Obama, political polarization

## Source Analysis (Critical for AP US History)

**Primary Sources:**
- Documents from the time period (speeches, letters, laws, images, etc.)
- Analyze author, audience, purpose, point of view, historical context

**HIPP Analysis:**
- **H**istorical Context: When/where was this created? What was happening?
- **I**ntended Audience: Who was the intended reader/viewer?
- **P**urpose: Why was this created? What did author want to achieve?
- **P**oint of View: What is author's perspective/bias?

---

## AP US History Exam Format

**Section I: Multiple Choice + Short Answer (95 minutes)**
- 55 multiple-choice questions (stimulus-based)
- 3 short-answer questions (analyze primary/secondary sources)

**Section II: Free Response (100 minutes, includes 15-minute reading period)**
- **Document-Based Question (DBQ):** Analyze 7 documents, develop argument with outside evidence
- **Long Essay Question (LEQ):** Choose 1 of 3 prompts, develop argument (no documents provided)

## Quick Reference

**Every US History Material Must:**
- Cover all major periods (colonial to present)
- Include primary source analysis
- Develop historical thinking skills (causation, CCOT, comparison)
- Prepare for state assessments/EOC exams
- (AP) Align to AP US History CED, practice DBQ/LEQ
- Connect to contemporary issues
- Include diverse perspectives (race, gender, class, region)
