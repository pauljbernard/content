# NYC DOE Curriculum Requirements and Priorities
**District:** New York City Department of Education
**Applies To:** All K-8 instructional materials
**Purpose:** District-specific initiatives beyond NYS standards

---

## Overview

NYC DOE has several **district-wide initiatives** that apply across all subjects and grade levels. Materials must align to New York State standards AND incorporate these NYC-specific priorities.

**Key Initiatives:**
1. Universal Literacy
2. Passport to Social Studies
3. NYC Science Scope & Sequence
4. Algebra for All (Grade 8)
5. Computer Science for All
6. Data-Informed Instruction

---

## 1. Universal Literacy

### NYC's Literacy Initiative (All Subjects)

**Core Principle:** Literacy is not just for ELA classâ€”every subject must develop reading, writing, speaking, and listening skills.

---

### Universal Literacy Components

#### Reading (All Subjects):

**Complex Text Exposure:**
- Grade-level or above texts in all subjects
- Informational texts in Math, Science, Social Studies
- Multiple text types (articles, primary sources, data sets, infographics)
- Close reading protocols applied across subjects

**Text-Dependent Questions:**
- Questions that require evidence from text
- Cannot be answered without reading the material
- Multiple DOK levels (1-4)

**Annotation Strategies:**
- Mark key information
- Note questions and connections
- Highlight evidence
- Code text (main idea, supporting detail, unknown vocabulary)

**Example in Math:**
Students read a word problem, annotate key information (underline knowns, circle question, box operation clues), then solve.

**Example in Science:**
Students read a passage about photosynthesis, annotate vocabulary, main idea, and evidence, then answer text-dependent questions.

---

#### Writing (All Subjects):

**Write to Learn:**
- Quick writes to process thinking
- Note-taking and summarizing
- Journals and reflections
- Explanation of reasoning

**Evidence-Based Arguments:**
- Claims supported by evidence
- Logical reasoning
- Cite sources and data
- Counterclaims (upper grades)

**Content-Area Writing:**
- Math: Explain solution strategies, justify answers
- Science: Lab reports, explanations of phenomena
- Social Studies: Document-based essays, argumentative writing

**Writing Process:**
- Prewriting (brainstorming, organizing)
- Drafting
- Revising (content, organization)
- Editing (conventions)
- Publishing/Presenting

---

#### Speaking and Listening (All Subjects):

**Academic Discussions:**
- Structured protocols (Think-Pair-Share, Socratic Seminar, etc.)
- Accountable talk norms
- Build on others' ideas
- Agree/disagree with evidence

**Collaborative Learning:**
- Partner work
- Small group investigations
- Peer review and feedback
- Jigsaw activities

**Presentations:**
- Share thinking and solutions
- Explain processes
- Teach peers
- Use academic language

---

#### Academic Language and Vocabulary (All Subjects):

**Tier 2 and Tier 3 Vocabulary:**
- **Tier 2:** Academic vocabulary across subjects (analyze, compare, justify)
- **Tier 3:** Domain-specific vocabulary (photosynthesis, denominator, democracy)

**Vocabulary Instruction:**
- Pre-teach key terms with student-friendly definitions
- Multiple exposures in context
- Word walls and anchor charts
- Morphology (roots, prefixes, suffixes)

**Language Functions:**
- Define, describe, explain, compare, argue, etc.
- Sentence frames for each function
- Practice in context

---

### Integration into Materials

**Math Materials Must Include:**
- Word problems requiring close reading
- Explain-your-thinking prompts
- Partner/group discussions
- Math vocabulary development
- Writing mathematical arguments

**Science Materials Must Include:**
- Informational text reading

- Academic discussions of phenomena
- Science vocabulary development

**Social Studies Materials Must Include:**
- Primary and secondary source reading
- Document analysis and annotation

## 2. Passport to Social Studies

### NYC's Social Studies Framework (K-8)

**Core Principles:**
- Inquiry-based learning (C3 Framework aligned)
- Document-Based Questions (DBQs)
- NYC and immigration focus
- Civic engagement

---

### Grade-Level Content Focus

**Kindergarten: Self and Others**
- My family and community
- Roles and responsibilities
- Past, present, future
- NYC neighborhoods

**Grade 1: My Family - Now and Long Ago**
- Family structures and traditions
- Changes over time
- NYC communities today and in the past
- Historical figures and everyday people

**Grade 2: My Community and Other US Communities**
- NYC geography (boroughs, neighborhoods)
- Urban, suburban, rural communities
- Comparing communities
- Community helpers and government

**Grade 3: New York City: Geography and Communities**
- NYC boroughs in depth
- Immigration to NYC
- Diverse communities and cultures
- NYC government and civics

**Grade 4: New York State and Local History**
- NYS geography and regions
- Native peoples of NYS
- Colonial period and Revolution
- Immigration to New York
- NYC and NYS connections

**Grade 5: United States History**
- Early peoples through Reconstruction
- NYC role in national history
- Immigration waves
- Constitutional foundations
- Westward expansion and sectionalism

**Grade 6: Eastern Hemisphere**
- Geography, cultures, and history
- Connections to NYC immigrant communities
- Focus on regions represented in NYC (Asia, Africa, Middle East, etc.)

**Grade 7: Western Hemisphere**
- Geography, cultures, and history
- Latin America and Caribbean focus
- Immigration to NYC from Western Hemisphere
- Contemporary issues

**Grade 8: United States History (Industrialization to Present)**
- Reconstruction through present day
- NYC in modern US history
- Immigration in 20th-21st centuries
- Civil rights and social movements
- Contemporary civics

---

### Passport to Social Studies Instructional Approaches

#### Inquiry-Based Learning:

**C3 Framework Inquiry Arc:**
1. Develop compelling and supporting questions
2. Apply disciplinary concepts (civics, economics, geography, history)
3. Evaluate sources and use evidence
4. Communicate conclusions and take informed action

**Example Compelling Question (Grade 3):**
"How has immigration shaped New York City's identity?"

**Supporting Questions:**
- Where did immigrants to NYC come from?
- Why did they come to NYC?
- How did they contribute to NYC culture?
- What challenges did they face?

---

#### Document-Based Questions (DBQs):

**NYC emphasizes DBQ approach:**

**Components:**
- Historical context (background information)
- Multiple documents (primary and secondary sources)
- Scaffolded questions for each document
- Essay task requiring synthesis of documents

**Document Types:**
- Written sources (speeches, letters, diary entries, articles)
- Visual sources (photographs, political cartoons, maps, artifacts)
- Data sources (charts, graphs, census data)

**Skills Developed:**
- Source analysis (author, audience, purpose, historical context)
- Corroboration (comparing sources)
- Close reading of historical texts
- Evidence-based argumentation

---

#### NYC Focus:

**All grades include NYC connections:**
- NYC geography, history, and culture
- Immigration and diversity
- NYC government and civics
- Current events in NYC
- Neighborhood studies

**Resources:**
- NYC DOE Passport to Social Studies curriculum maps
- Document sets for NYC history
- NYC museum partnerships
- Community field studies

---

#### Civic Engagement:

**Students take informed action:**
- Identify community issues
- Research and analyze
- Develop solutions
- Present to authentic audiences
- Participate in civic processes

**Examples:**
- Grade 3: Improve our school/neighborhood project
- Grade 5: Historic preservation advocacy
- Grade 8: Contemporary issue research and action plan

## 4. Algebra for All (Grade 8)

### NYC Initiative: All Grade 8 Students Take Algebra

**Goal:** Ensure all students are on track for advanced math in high school

---

### Requirements:

**Content:**
- Algebra I curriculum in Grade 8
- NYS Regents Algebra I exam (high school exam)
- Pre-algebra in Grade 7 (prerequisite)

**Support:**
- Universal screening in Grade 6-7
- Intervention for students below grade level
- Summer bridge programs
- Extended time and tutoring

**Materials Implication:**
- Grade 7 materials must prepare for Algebra in Grade 8
- Grade 8 materials must be Regents Algebra I aligned
- Robust differentiation for students entering below grade level

## 6. Data-Informed Instruction

### NYC Emphasis on Using Data to Drive Instruction

**Data Sources:**
- NYS assessments (grades 3-8)
- NYC periodic assessments (benchmark/interim)
- Formative assessments (ongoing)
- Student work analysis
- Observational data

---

### Data Use Cycle:

**1. Collect Data:**
- Administer assessments
- Gather student work
- Track progress

**2. Analyze Data:**
- Identify trends and patterns
- Determine strengths and gaps
- Disaggregate by student groups

**3. Plan Instruction:**
- Adjust curriculum pacing
- Plan interventions and extensions
- Group students strategically
- Differentiate instruction

**4. Teach and Monitor:**
- Implement adjusted instruction
- Monitor student progress
- Collect formative data

**5. Reflect and Adjust:**
- Evaluate effectiveness
- Make further adjustments
- Continuous improvement

---

### Materials Implication:

**Assessments:**
- Formative assessments embedded throughout
- Interim/benchmark assessments aligned to NYS tests
- Data reports and analysis tools for teachers

**Differentiation:**
- Intervention resources for students below grade level
- Extension/enrichment for advanced students
- Flexible grouping strategies

**Professional Learning:**
- Data analysis protocols
- Using data to inform instruction
- Intervention planning

## Integration Example: Grade 5 Math Lesson

**Content:** Adding fractions with unlike denominators

**NYC Requirements Integration:**

**1. Universal Literacy:**
- Start with word problem requiring close reading
- Annotate problem (underline knowns, circle question, box operation clues)
- Write explanation of solution strategy
- Partner discussion using academic language ("I found a common denominator by...")

**2. Data-Informed Instruction:**
- Begin with Quick Check from previous lesson (analyze data)
- Differentiate based on results (intervention group, on-level, extension)
- Formative assessment at end (inform next lesson)

**3. Multilingual Learners:**
- Sentence frames for explanations (5 proficiency levels)
- Visual fraction models
- Bilingual glossary (common denominator, equivalent, simplify)

**4. Culturally Responsive:**
- Word problem context: "Maria's family is planning a potluck dinner in their Bronx neighborhood..."
- Diverse student names and contexts throughout
- Connection to students' lives

**5. CS Integration (where appropriate):**
- Optional: Use fraction app to explore equivalents
- Digital fraction models
- Code a fraction calculator (extension)
