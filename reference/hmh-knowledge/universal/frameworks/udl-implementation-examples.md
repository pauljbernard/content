# UDL Implementation Examples
**Practical Applications of Universal Design for Learning**
**Scope:** Universal (all subjects and districts)
**Audience:** Curriculum developers implementing UDL principles

---

## Overview

This guide provides **concrete examples** of how to implement UDL (Universal Design for Learning) principles in curriculum design. While UDL principles are described in `/universal/frameworks/udl-principles-guide.md`, this guide shows **how to apply them** in practice.

**Core Principle:** UDL removes barriers to learning by providing multiple means of engagement, representation, and action/expression.

---

## Quick UDL Recap

### The 3 Principles:

**1. Multiple Means of Engagement (WHY of learning)**
- Recruit interest
- Sustain effort and persistence
- Support self-regulation

**2. Multiple Means of Representation (WHAT of learning)**
- Provide options for perception
- Provide options for language and symbols
- Provide options for comprehension

**3. Multiple Means of Action & Expression (HOW of learning)**
- Provide options for physical action
- Provide options for expression and communication
- Provide options for executive functions

---

## Principle 1: Multiple Means of Engagement Examples

### Checkpoint 1.1: Recruit Interest

**Goal:** Offer choices that reflect learner interests

**Example 1: Math Word Problems with Choice**
```
Choose ONE problem to solve:

Option A (Sports): A basketball team scored 45 points in the first half
and 52 points in the second half. What was their total score?

Option B (Music): A concert had 45 people in the first row and 52 people
in the second row. How many people total?

Option C (Animals): A zoo has 45 mammals and 52 birds. How many animals
total?
```

**Why It Works:** Same mathematical goal (addition), different contexts appeal to different interests.

---

**Example 2: ELA - Text Selection Choice**
```
Today we're practicing making inferences. Choose ONE text to read:

□ "The Soccer Game" (Sports narrative)
□ "The Science Fair" (STEM narrative)
□ "The Art Show" (Arts narrative)

All three texts require the same inference skills.
```

**Why It Works:** Students select based on interest, increasing engagement.

---

### Checkpoint 1.2: Sustain Effort and Persistence

**Goal:** Provide scaffolding and support that can be gradually released

**Example 1: Graduated Problem Difficulty**
```
Warmup (Easy): 12 + 15 = ?
Practice 1 (Medium): 123 + 145 = ?
Practice 2 (Medium-Hard): 1,234 + 1,456 = ?
Challenge (Hard): 12,345 + 14,567 = ?

Start where you feel comfortable. Try to complete at least 3 problems.
```

**Why It Works:** Students choose entry point, experience success, build toward challenge.

---

**Example 2: Scaffolded Writing with Optional Supports**
```
Writing Task: Explain your solution strategy.

Need help getting started?
□ Use sentence frames: "First I ___. Then I ___."
□ Use this outline: [1. What you did, 2. Why, 3. Result]
□ Look at this example: [Sample explanation provided]

All supports are optional. Use what helps you!
```

**Why It Works:** Support available but not forced; students self-select.

---

### Checkpoint 1.3: Support Self-Regulation

**Goal:** Help students monitor their own learning

**Example 1: Self-Assessment Checklist**
```
Before Submitting, Check:
□ I read the problem carefully
□ I identified what the question asks
□ I showed my work
□ I checked my answer
□ I explained my thinking

How confident am I? [Not confident - Somewhat - Very confident]
```

**Why It Works:** Students develop metacognitive awareness.

---

**Example 2: Progress Tracker**
```
Unit 3: Fractions
□ Lesson 1: Identify fractions - Completed
□ Lesson 2: Equivalent fractions - In Progress
□ Lesson 3: Compare fractions - Not started yet
□ Lesson 4: Add/subtract - Not started yet

I'm making progress! Next up: Comparing fractions.
```

**Why It Works:** Visual progress builds motivation and self-regulation.

## Principle 2: Multiple Means of Representation Examples

### Checkpoint 2.1: Options for Perception

**Goal:** Offer information in multiple sensory modalities

**Example 1: Multi-Modal Vocabulary Instruction**
```
New Word: "Quotient" (the answer to a division problem)

□ Visual: [Image showing 12 ÷ 3 = 4, with "4" labeled as quotient]
□ Auditory: [Audio pronunciation + definition]
□ Kinesthetic: [Manipulatives to show division resulting in quotient]
□ Written: Definition in text

All modes provided simultaneously.
```

**Why It Works:** Students can access through preferred modality or multiple modalities.

---

**Example 2: Video with Multiple Access Points**
```
Instructional Video: "How to Add Fractions"

Options:
□ Play with audio (hear explanation)
□ Captions ON (read along)
□ Transcript available (read without video)
□ Adjustable playback speed (0.75x, 1x, 1.25x, 1.5x)
```

**Why It Works:** Removes perceptual barriers.

---

### Checkpoint 2.2: Options for Language and Symbols

**Goal:** Clarify vocabulary, syntax, and symbols

**Example 1: Vocabulary Support Menu**
```
Problem: "Calculate the perimeter of the rectangle."

Need help with vocabulary?
□ Glossary: [Click "perimeter" for definition]
□ Visual: [Diagram showing perimeter highlighted]
□ Spanish cognate: "perímetro"
□ Simple language: "Distance around the outside"
```

**Why It Works:** Multiple ways to access word meaning.

---

**Example 2: Math Problem with Language Scaffolds**
```
Original: "A gardener plants 3 rows of tulips with 12 tulips in each row.
How many tulips does the gardener plant in total?"

Simplified Language Option:
"There are 3 rows. Each row has 12 tulips. How many tulips total?"

Visual Option: [Image showing 3 rows of 12 tulips]

Both options lead to same mathematical goal: 3 × 12
```

**Why It Works:** Language complexity doesn't obscure mathematical content.

---

### Checkpoint 2.3: Options for Comprehension

**Goal:** Activate background knowledge, highlight patterns, guide processing

**Example 1: Activating Prior Knowledge**
```
Today's Lesson: Multiplying Fractions

Before we start, let's review:
□ What do you remember about fractions?
□ What does multiplication mean?
□ Can you draw a model showing 3 × 4?

Quick Review Video (optional): [3-minute fraction basics]
```

**Why It Works:** Connects new learning to existing knowledge.

---

**Example 2: Concept Map Provided**
```
Unit: Fractions

Visual Concept Map:
[Fractions] connects to:
  → [Equivalent Fractions] (same value, different form)
  → [Comparing Fractions] (which is larger?)
  → [Operations with Fractions] (add, subtract, multiply, divide)
    → [Today: Adding with Unlike Denominators]

You are here: ↑
```

**Why It Works:** Shows how concepts relate, provides organization.

## Principle 3: Multiple Means of Action & Expression Examples

### Checkpoint 3.1: Options for Physical Action

**Goal:** Vary methods for response and navigation

**Example 1: Assessment Response Options**
```
Question 5: Explain your strategy.

Choose how to respond:
□ Type your explanation
□ Write by hand and upload photo
□ Draw and label a diagram
□ Record audio explanation (speak your answer)
□ Create a video showing your work
```

**Why It Works:** Removes barriers related to writing speed, motor skills, or language output.

---

**Example 2: Interactive vs. Paper-Based**
```
Practice Problems Available:
□ Digital interactive (drag-and-drop, instant feedback)
□ Printable worksheet (work on paper)
□ Oral practice with partner
□ Manipulatives (physical objects)

Choose your preferred format.
```

**Why It Works:** Students work in format that suits their needs.

---

### Checkpoint 3.2: Options for Expression and Communication

**Goal:** Provide multiple media for communication

**Example 1: "Show What You Know" Options**
```
Demonstrate your understanding of equivalent fractions:

Choose at least 2:
□ Write an explanation
□ Draw visual models
□ Create a poster
□ Make a video tutorial
□ Teach a partner (teacher observes)
□ Complete problem set
□ Build with manipulatives and explain
```

**Why It Works:** Students express understanding through strengths.

---

**Example 2: Math Journal with Scaffolds**
```
Reflection: What did you learn about division today?

Options:
□ Write in complete sentences
□ Draw and label
□ Use bullet points
□ Use sentence frames: "I learned ___. This connects to ___."
□ Record audio reflection
```

**Why It Works:** Focus on content, not writing format.

---

### Checkpoint 3.3: Options for Executive Functions

**Goal:** Support planning, strategy development, managing information

**Example 1: Problem-Solving Checklist**
```
Word Problem Strategy:

□ Step 1: Read the problem
□ Step 2: Identify what you know
□ Step 3: Identify what you need to find
□ Step 4: Choose a strategy
□ Step 5: Solve
□ Step 6: Check your answer

Use this checklist every time!
```

**Why It Works:** Structures complex process into manageable steps.

---

**Example 2: Graphic Organizer for Planning**
```
Writing an Explanation:

[Box 1: What I Did First]
↓
[Box 2: What I Did Next]
↓
[Box 3: My Final Answer]
↓
[Box 4: Why It Makes Sense]

Fill in each box, then write your paragraph.
```

**Why It Works:** Visual organization supports planning.

## Combined UDL Examples (Multiple Principles)

### Example 1: Universally Designed Math Task

**Task:** Solve multiplication word problems

**Engagement (Multiple Interest Options):**
- Choose context: Sports, Cooking, Shopping, or Animals

**Representation (Multiple Formats):**
- Visual diagram provided
- Key vocabulary defined in glossary
- Video model of similar problem (optional)

**Action/Expression (Multiple Response Methods):**
- Show work on paper, digitally, or with manipulatives
- Explain verbally or in writing
- Use optional sentence frames

**Result:** All students can access and demonstrate learning.

---

### Example 2: Universally Designed Reading Lesson

**Task:** Make inferences from text

**Engagement:**
- Choice of 3 texts at same complexity (different topics)
- Partner or independent reading option

**Representation:**
- Text available in digital and print
- Audio narration available
- Vocabulary pre-taught with visuals
- Annotation guide provided

**Action/Expression:**
- Written response, drawn response, or oral presentation
- Sentence frames optional
- Thinking map provided for organizing

**Result:** Multiple entry points, all leading to same learning goal.

## UDL in Digital vs. Print

### Digital Advantages:
- Text-to-speech available
- Adjustable font size/spacing
- Interactive elements
- Instant feedback

### Print Advantages:
- No technology barriers
- Tactile interaction
- No screen fatigue
- Easier annotation for some

**UDL Solution:** Provide both, design for parity (same learning opportunity in each format)

## UDL Quick Design Questions

### When Designing Any Learning Experience:

**Engagement:**
- [ ] Have I provided choices?
- [ ] Have I built in supports for persistence?
- [ ] Can students self-monitor progress?

**Representation:**
- [ ] Is information available in multiple formats?
- [ ] Are vocabulary and symbols clarified?
- [ ] Have I activated prior knowledge?

**Action/Expression:**
- [ ] Can students respond in multiple ways?
- [ ] Have I provided executive function supports?
- [ ] Can students demonstrate learning through different media?

## Common UDL Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Mistake 1: Providing ONE Alternative

**Problem:** "Here's the worksheet. If you can't do it, here's an easier version."

**Fix:** Provide multiple options for all students, not just "accommodations" for some.

---

### ❌ Mistake 2: Lowering Expectations

**Problem:** "Students who use scaffolds get easier questions."

**Fix:** Same rigorous goal, multiple paths to reach it.

---

### ❌ Mistake 3: Over-Choice

**Problem:** 20 different options overwhelm students.

**Fix:** 2-4 meaningful choices.

---

### ❌ Mistake 4: Separating by Ability

**Problem:** "Advanced students do Project A, struggling students do Worksheet B."

**Fix:** All students choose from same options; supports available to all.

The goal is to make expert learners—purposeful, motivated, resourceful, knowledgeable, strategic, and goal-directed.
