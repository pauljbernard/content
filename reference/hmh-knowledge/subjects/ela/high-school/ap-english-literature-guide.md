# AP English Literature and Composition Guide
**Course Level:** Typically Grade 12 (or Grade 11)
**Prerequisites:** English I, English II, English III (strong reading and writing skills)
**Applies To:** AP English Literature and Composition courses

---

## Overview

AP English Literature and Composition is a college-level course focusing on close reading, literary analysis, and interpretation of poetry, prose fiction, and drama. Students engage with sophisticated literary texts, analyze how authors use literary elements and techniques, and develop critical writing skills for literary analysis.

**College/Career Readiness:** AP Lit prepares students for college literature courses, humanities, and critical analysis across disciplines. AP exam scores of 3+ often earn college credit for introductory literature courses. The course develops close reading, analytical thinking, and interpretive writingâ€”skills essential for law, humanities, publishing, education, and any field requiring textual analysis.

**AP Exam:** May exam with multiple-choice section (poetry and prose analysis) and free-response section (poetry analysis, prose fiction analysis, literary argument essays).

---

## Course Focus

**Primary Focus:** **Literary Analysis** - Close reading and interpretation of fiction, poetry, and drama

**Key Distinctions from AP Language:**
- **AP Lit:** Fiction, poetry, drama; literary analysis; literary elements and techniques
- **AP Lang:** Nonfiction (essays, speeches); rhetoric and argument; rhetorical strategies

**Student Skills Developed:**
1. **Close Reading** - Analyze literary texts with precision and depth
2. **Literary Analysis** - Interpret how authors use literary elements and techniques
3. **Poetry Analysis** - Analyze poetic form, structure, and figurative language
4. **Prose Fiction Analysis** - Analyze narrative techniques, character, theme
5. **Comparative Analysis** - Compare texts and interpretations
6. **Literary Argumentation** - Defend interpretations with textual evidence
7. **Sophisticated Writing** - Craft nuanced, evidence-based literary essays

## Key Literary Concepts

### 1. Character (CHR)

**Characterization:**
- Direct characterization (author tells us about character)
- Indirect characterization (STEAL: Speech, Thoughts, Effect on others, Actions, Looks)

**Character Types:**
- Protagonist vs. antagonist
- Round vs. flat
- Dynamic (changes) vs. static (doesn't change)
- Archetypal characters

**Character Function:**
- Drive plot forward
- Reveal themes
- Create conflict
- Serve as foil (contrasting character that highlights another's traits)

**Analysis Focus:**
- How does author develop character?
- How does character contribute to meaning?
- How do contrasts between characters create significance?

### 2. Setting (SET)

**Elements of Setting:**
- Time (historical period, season, time of day)
- Place (geographic location, physical environment)
- Social/cultural context

**Function of Setting:**
- Create mood and atmosphere
- Reflect or contrast with characters' internal states
- Symbolize themes
- Create conflict (person vs. environment)
- Establish historical/cultural context

**Analysis Focus:**
- How does author establish setting?
- How does setting contribute to meaning?
- How does setting interact with character and theme?

---

### 3. Structure (STR)

**Elements of Structure:**
- Plot structure (exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution)
- Narrative structure (chronological, non-linear, frame narrative, in medias res)
- Stanza structure (poetry)
- Scene structure (drama)
- Sentence and paragraph structure (syntax, diction)

**Contrasts and Juxtapositions:**
- Before/after
- Past/present
- Light/dark, life/death, innocence/experience
- Contrasting characters, settings, events

**Function of Structure:**
- Create tension, suspense, surprise
- Emphasize themes through organization
- Control pacing and emphasis
- Create meaning through contrasts

**Analysis Focus:**
- How is text organized?
- How does structure create emphasis or meaning?
- How do contrasts contribute to significance?

---

### 4. Narration (NAR)

**Point of View:**
- **First person:** "I" narrator (participant in story)
- **Second person:** "You" narrator (rare, addresses reader)
- **Third person limited:** Outside narrator, one character's perspective
- **Third person omniscient:** Outside narrator, all-knowing
- **Multiple narrators:** Shifting perspectives

**Narrator Reliability:**
- Reliable narrator (trustworthy, accurate)
- Unreliable narrator (biased, naÃ¯ve, untruthful, mentally unstable)

**Function of Narration:**
- Control information (what reader knows/doesn't know)
- Create dramatic irony (reader knows more than character)
- Establish tone and perspective
- Reveal character and theme

**Speaker in Poetry:**
- Speaker â‰  poet (unless explicitly autobiographical)
- Speaker's perspective, tone, situation
- Relationship between speaker and subject/audience

**Analysis Focus:**
- Who is narrator/speaker?
- How reliable?
- How does narration control information and create meaning?

---

### 5. Figurative Language (FIG)

**Types of Figurative Language:**
- **Simile:** Comparison using "like" or "as"
- **Metaphor:** Direct comparison (A is B)
- **Extended metaphor:** Sustained comparison throughout passage or poem
- **Personification:** Giving human qualities to non-human things
- **Hyperbole:** Exaggeration for emphasis
- **Understatement:** Deliberate downplaying for effect
- **Paradox:** Seemingly contradictory statement that reveals truth
- **Oxymoron:** Contradictory words together ("bitter sweet")

**Imagery:**
- Sensory details (visual, auditory, tactile, olfactory, gustatory)
- Creates vivid mental pictures
- Appeals to senses

**Symbolism:**
- Object, character, or event represents abstract idea
- Conventional symbols (light = knowledge, darkness = ignorance)
- Contextual symbols (unique to specific text)

**Irony:**
- **Verbal:** Saying opposite of what is meant (sarcasm)
- **Situational:** Outcome opposite of expectation
- **Dramatic:** Audience knows something character doesn't

**Function of Figurative Language:**
- Create vivid images and sensory experience
- Convey abstract ideas concretely
- Establish tone and mood
- Reveal theme
- Create layers of meaning

**Analysis Focus:**
- What figurative language is present?
- How does it create meaning?
- How does it contribute to theme, tone, characterization?

## Genre Study

### Poetry Analysis

**Poetic Form and Structure:**
- **Stanza:** Group of lines (couplet, tercet, quatrain, etc.)
- **Line:** Single row of words
- **Rhyme scheme:** Pattern of rhymes (ABAB, AABB, etc.)
- **Meter:** Rhythmic pattern (iambic pentameter, etc.)
- **Free verse:** No regular rhyme or meter

**Poetic Forms:**
- **Sonnet:** 14 lines
  - Shakespearean (ABAB CDCD EFEF GG)
  - Petrarchan (ABBAABBA CDECDE or CDCDCD)
- **Villanelle:** 19 lines, specific rhyme and repetition pattern
- **Haiku:** 5-7-5 syllables (Japanese form)
- **Ode:** Lyric poem, serious tone, elevated subject
- **Elegy:** Poem of mourning
- **Ballad:** Narrative poem, often with musical quality
- **Epic:** Long narrative poem about heroic deeds

**Sound Devices:**
- Alliteration, assonance, consonance
- Onomatopoeia
- Rhyme (end, internal, slant)
- Repetition and refrain

**Poetic Techniques:**
- **Enjambment:** Line continues to next line without pause
- **End-stopped line:** Line ends with punctuation (pause)
- **Caesura:** Pause within line (punctuation mid-line)
- **Apostrophe:** Address to absent person or abstract concept

**Analysis Focus:**
- How does form contribute to meaning?
- How do sound devices create effects?
- How does imagery create tone and theme?
- What is speaker's attitude and situation?

### Prose Fiction Analysis

**Narrative Techniques:**

- Theme

**Types of Prose Fiction:**
- **Novel:** Extended narrative
- **Novella:** Shorter than novel, longer than short story
- **Short story:** Brief narrative, focused plot and characters

**Analysis Focus:**
- How does author develop characters?
- How does narrative technique shape meaning?
- How do setting and structure contribute to theme?
- What is significance of symbols and motifs?

### Drama Analysis

**Dramatic Elements:**
- Dialogue and monologue
- Stage directions
- Soliloquy (character speaks thoughts aloud when alone)
- Aside (character speaks to audience)
- Acts and scenes

**Dramatic Techniques:**
- Dramatic irony
- Tragic hero and tragic flaw (hamartia)
- Comic relief
- Foil characters

**Analysis Focus:**
- How does dialogue reveal character?

**Common Dramatic Works in AP Lit:**

## AP English Literature Essay Types

### 1. Poetry Analysis (Question 1)

**Prompt Format:**
- Provides poem (often 20th/21st century)
- Asks you to analyze how poet uses literary elements and techniques to convey meaning/develop theme/create effect

**Task:**
- Close read poem
- Identify literary elements and techniques (figurative language, imagery, structure, sound devices, etc.)
- Explain HOW these contribute to meaning/theme/effect

**Essay Structure:**
- Introduction: Introduce poem, poet (if known), thesis about how elements create meaning
- Body paragraphs: Analyze specific literary techniques with evidence
- Conclusion: Overall significance

**Analysis Approach:**
- Annotate poem (mark imagery, figurative language, structure, sound devices, shifts)
- Identify speaker, situation, subject, tone
- Determine theme or central meaning
- Analyze how techniques create this meaning

**Strong Thesis Example:**
> In "The Fish," Elizabeth Bishop uses vivid visual imagery, personification, and a shift in perspective to transform a mundane fishing experience into a revelation about beauty, age, and respect.

**Key Success Strategies:**
- Read poem multiple times
- Don't just identify techniquesâ€”analyze FUNCTION and EFFECT
- Integrate short quotations smoothly
- Explain how techniques contribute to meaning

---

### 2. Prose Fiction Analysis (Question 2)

**Prompt Format:**
- Provides prose passage (excerpt from novel or short story)
- Asks you to analyze how author uses literary elements and techniques to convey meaning/develop character/create theme

**Task:**
- Close read passage
- Identify narrative techniques (characterization, point of view, imagery, diction, syntax, etc.)
- Explain HOW these create meaning/develop character/establish theme

**Essay Structure:**
- Introduction: Introduce passage, author (if known), context, thesis
- Body paragraphs: Analyze specific techniques with evidence
- Conclusion: Overall significance

**Analysis Approach:**
- Annotate passage (mark characterization, imagery, tone shifts, symbols)
- Identify narrative perspective, characters, conflict
- Determine what passage reveals (character development, theme, relationship, etc.)
- Analyze how author's choices create this revelation

**Strong Thesis Example:**
> In this passage from Their Eyes Were Watching God, Zora Neale Hurston uses natural imagery, shifts in narrative distance, and dialect to reveal Janie's awakening to romantic love and her emerging sense of self.

**Key Success Strategies:**
- Identify what the passage is ABOUT (character, relationship, theme)
- Analyze narrative techniques systematically
- Use specific textual evidence
- Explain how techniques create effects

---

### 3. Literary Argument (Question 3)

**Prompt Format:**
- Presents literary concept, theme, or technique
- Provides list of suggested works (or asks you to choose your own)
- Asks you to write essay analyzing how a work of "literary merit" explores this concept

**Task:**
- Choose work you know well (novel, play, epic poemâ€”substantial work)
- Develop claim about how work addresses prompt
- Support with specific textual evidence
- Demonstrate deep knowledge of work

**Essay Structure:**
- Introduction: Introduce work and author, thesis addressing prompt
- Body paragraphs: Develop argument with specific evidence from work
- Conclusion: Significance or broader implications

**Choosing a Work:**
- Choose work you know VERY well (studied in class, read multiple times)
- Must be "literary merit" (complex, sophisticated, recognized literary value)
- Must fit prompt (don't force a work that doesn't fit)
- Have 3-5 "go-to" works you know deeply

**Strong Thesis Example:**
- Prompt: "In many works, a character's sense of isolation leads to self-discovery."
- Thesis: "In Invisible Man, Ralph Ellison uses the protagonist's literal and metaphorical invisibilityâ€”his social isolation as a Black man in mid-20th-century Americaâ€”to drive his journey from naÃ¯vetÃ© to radical self-awareness and rejection of imposed identities."

**Key Success Strategies:**
- Choose works strategically (know them deeply)
- Use specific textual evidence (plot details, quotations, character names, etc.)
- Stay focused on prompt (answer the question)
- Demonstrate sophistication (avoid plot summary, analyze significance)

## Literary Argument and Close Reading Skills

### Developing a Literary Thesis

**Strong Literary Thesis:**
- Arguable (not just fact or plot summary)
- Specific (focused on particular aspect of text)
- Insightful (reveals meaningful interpretation)
- Addresses prompt directly

**Example:**
- Weak: "The Great Gatsby is about the American Dream." (too broad, obvious)
- Weak: "Gatsby loves Daisy and wants to be with her." (plot summary, not arguable)
- Strong: "Through Gatsby's obsessive pursuit of an idealized past, Fitzgerald reveals the American Dream's transformation from a vision of self-creation to a destructive fantasy fueled by materialism and nostalgia."

---

### Using Textual Evidence

**Types of Evidence:**
- Direct quotations (brief, integrated)
- Paraphrase (summary of plot points or character actions)
- Specific references (character names, symbols, events)

**Integrating Quotations:**
- Introduce context
- Embed quotation smoothly (grammatically)
- Cite correctly (if line numbers or page numbers provided)
- Analyze and explain significance (don't just drop quotes)

**Example:**
> When Fitzgerald describes Daisy's voice as "full of money" (120), he reveals how her allure is inextricable from wealth and class, exposing Gatsby's romantic ideal as fundamentally materialistic.

---

### Line of Reasoning

**Coherent Argument:**
- Each paragraph builds on previous
- Topic sentences connect to thesis
- Transitions show logical progression
- Overall essay develops unified interpretation

---

### Commentary and Analysis

**Commentary:**
- Explain HOW and WHY evidence supports claim
- Analyze techniques and their effects
- Connect evidence to theme/meaning

**Avoid:**
- Plot summary without analysis
- "Laundry list" of devices without explaining function
- Stating obvious without interpretation

## Preparing for the AP Exam

### AP Exam Format

**Section I: Multiple Choice (55 questions, 60 minutes)**
- 2-3 poetry passages
- 2-3 prose fiction passages
- Questions on literary analysis, interpretation, techniques
- 45% of exam score

**Section II: Free Response (3 essays, 2 hours)**
- **Question 1: Poetry Analysis** (40 minutes suggested, 16.67% of score)
- **Question 2: Prose Fiction Analysis** (40 minutes suggested, 16.67% of score)
- **Question 3: Literary Argument** (40 minutes suggested, 16.67% of score)

**Total Exam:** 3 hours

---

### Scoring Rubrics (0-6 scale for each essay)

**6-5 (Effective):**
- Defensible thesis
- Specific evidence well-integrated
- Clear line of reasoning
- Sophisticated commentary
- Vivid and persuasive style
- (For Q3) Deep knowledge of chosen work

**4-3 (Adequate):**
- Defensible thesis
- Adequate evidence
- Line of reasoning present
- Adequate commentary
- (For Q3) Adequate knowledge of chosen work

**2-1 (Inadequate):**
- Weak or unclear thesis
- Insufficient or irrelevant evidence
- Weak line of reasoning
- Limited commentary
- (For Q3) Limited knowledge of chosen work

---

### Exam Preparation Strategies

**Throughout the Year:**
- Read literary texts regularly (poetry, prose, drama)
- Practice close reading and annotation
- Write all three essay types multiple times
- Build "bank" of 3-5 works you know deeply for Question 3
- Study literary terms and techniques

**Before the Exam:**
- Review literary terms
- Practice timed essays (40 minutes each)
- Review released AP exam questions and scoring commentary (College Board website)
- Reread/review major works for Question 3
- Practice poetry and prose close reading

**Exam Day Strategies:**
- **Time Management:** ~40 minutes per essay (5-10 min close reading/planning, 25-30 min writing, 5 min proofreading)
- **Multiple Choice:** Skip difficult questions, return later; focus on process of elimination
- **Poetry Analysis:** Read 3-4 times, annotate heavily
- **Prose Fiction Analysis:** Annotate, identify narrator and characters
- **Literary Argument:** Choose work you know BEST, even if not on suggested list
