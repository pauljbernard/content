# High School Composition Guide
**Grades:** 9-12
**Applies To:** All high school writing instruction across all grades and courses

---

## Overview

High school composition develops college-level writing skills across multiple genres: argument, informative/explanatory, narrative, and research. Students learn to craft sophisticated, well-supported writing for diverse purposes and audiences with clarity, coherence, and style.

**College/Career Readiness:** Strong writing skills are REQUIRED for college success across all majors and essential for nearly every career. High school writing must prepare students for college-level composition, research writing, and professional communication.

**Typical HS Writing Emphases:**
- **Grade 9:** Narrative, literary analysis, introduction to argument
- **Grade 10:** Informative/explanatory, argument, comparative analysis
- **Grade 11:** Argument (rhetoric), research, synthesis (often aligned with American history/culture)
- **Grade 12:** Argument, research, college application essays, senior projects

**AP Options:**
- **AP English Language and Composition** (Grade 11-12): Rhetoric, argument, analysis of nonfiction
- **AP English Literature and Composition** (Grade 11-12): Literary analysis essays

---

## Core Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

1. **Argument Writing** - Construct evidence-based arguments with claims, counterclaims, and logical reasoning
2. **Informative/Explanatory Writing** - Convey complex information clearly and accurately
3. **Narrative Writing** - Craft narratives with coherent structure, detail, and reflection
4. **Research Writing** - Conduct research, synthesize sources, document correctly
5. **Rhetorical Analysis** - Analyze how authors use rhetoric to achieve purpose
6. **Writing Process** - Plan, draft, revise, edit, and publish effectively
7. **Style and Voice** - Develop sophisticated style, sentence variety, and author's voice
8. **Grammar and Conventions** - Use standard English conventions correctly and effectively
9. **Digital and Multimodal Composition** - Create digital texts, presentations, and multimedia

## Key Writing Modes

### 1. Argument Writing (Grades 9-12, Emphasis in 11-12)

**Purpose:** Persuade audience to accept a position or take action

**Key Components:**

**Claim/Thesis:**
- Clear, debatable position on an issue
- Arguable (not fact or opinion without support)
- Specific and focused

**Evidence:**
- Facts, statistics, expert testimony, examples, anecdotes
- Relevant and sufficient to support claim
- From credible sources (cite correctly)

**Reasoning:**
- Logical connections between evidence and claim
- Warrants (explain WHY evidence supports claim)
- Anticipate objections

**Counterclaim and Rebuttal:**
- Acknowledge opposing viewpoints
- Refute or concede and qualify
- Strengthens argument by showing fairness and thorough thinking

**Structure:**
- Introduction with claim/thesis
- Body paragraphs (each with sub-claim, evidence, reasoning)
- Counterclaim paragraph (acknowledge and refute)
- Conclusion (reinforce claim, call to action, broader implications)

---

**Rhetorical Appeals (Aristotelian):**

**Logos (Logic):**
- Logical reasoning, evidence, facts, statistics
- Deductive reasoning (general â†’ specific)
- Inductive reasoning (specific â†’ general)

**Ethos (Credibility):**
- Establish author's credibility and authority
- Demonstrate knowledge, fairness, good character
- Cite expert sources

**Pathos (Emotion):**
- Appeal to audience's emotions, values, beliefs
- Use vivid language, anecdotes, examples
- Create connection with audience

**Balance:** Effective arguments use ALL three appeals appropriately

---

**Common Logical Fallacies (Teach to Avoid):**
- Ad hominem (attack person, not argument)
- Straw man (misrepresent opposing view)
- False dilemma (only two options when more exist)
- Slippery slope (chain reaction without evidence)
- Hasty generalization (conclusion from insufficient evidence)
- Circular reasoning (claim as evidence for itself)
- Appeal to popularity (bandwagon)
- Red herring (irrelevant distraction)

### 2. Informative/Explanatory Writing (All Grades)

**Purpose:** Convey complex information clearly and accurately, explain concepts, analyze ideas

**Key Components:**

**Clear Focus:**
- Controlling idea or thesis (for analytical writing)
- Clear topic (for informational writing)

**Organization:**
- Logical structure (chronological, cause-effect, problem-solution, compare-contrast, classification)
- Clear transitions between ideas
- Each paragraph unified around one main idea

**Development:**
- Relevant facts, definitions, concrete details, quotations, examples
- Sufficient information to thoroughly explain topic
- Precise language and domain-specific vocabulary

**Objective Tone:**
- Formal, objective language
- Avoid first person (I, we) in formal writing
- Balanced, impartial presentation

---

**Types of Informative/Explanatory Writing:**

**Expository Essay:**
- Explain topic, concept, or process
- Example: "How does photosynthesis work?"

**Analytical Essay:**
- Analyze text, concept, or phenomenon
- Literary analysis, rhetorical analysis, cause-effect analysis
- Example: "Analyze the causes of the Civil War"

**Definition Essay:**
- Define abstract concept with examples and analysis
- Example: "What is justice?"

**Compare-Contrast Essay:**
- Analyze similarities and differences
- Point-by-point or block organization

**Process Essay:**
- Explain how something works or how to do something
- Step-by-step, chronological

**Research Report:**
- Present findings from research
- Objective, informative, well-sourced

---

**Real-World Applications:**
- Research papers (college, professional)
- Technical writing (instructions, reports)
- Journalism (news articles, feature stories)
- Business writing (reports, memos)
- Science writing (lab reports, explanations)

**Career Connections:**
- Technical Writing (manuals, documentation)
- Journalism (reporting, feature writing)
- Science Communication (explaining research to public)

### 3. Narrative Writing (All Grades, Emphasis in 9-10)

**Purpose:** Tell a story, convey experience, explore identity and meaning

**Key Components:**

**Narrative Techniques:**
- Point of view (first person most common in personal narrative)
- Dialogue (authentic, purposeful)
- Pacing (balance action and reflection)
- Sensory details (show, don't tell)
- Vivid description

**Structure:**
- Clear sequence of events
- Plot structure (exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution)
- Or: Begin in medias res, use flashback
- Engaging introduction (hook reader)
- Satisfying conclusion (resolution, reflection, insight)

**Characterization:**
- Develop characters (appearance, actions, thoughts, dialogue, interactions)
- Dynamic characters (change over time)

**Setting:**
- Time and place
- Create vivid sense of setting through description
- Setting impacts mood and events

**Theme/Reflection:**
- Significance of events (what did narrator learn/realize?)
- Insight or reflection (especially in personal narrative)

---

**Types of Narrative Writing:**

**Personal Narrative:**
- True story from writer's life
- First person, reflective
- Reveals something meaningful about writer's identity, growth, or understanding

**Memoir:**
- Extended personal narrative
- Focus on particular period, theme, or aspect of life
- Reflection on significance

**Short Story:**
- Fictional narrative
- Creative writing, imaginative
- Apply literary techniques studied in literature

**College Application Essay:**
- Specific type of personal narrative
- Tell story that reveals character, values, growth
- Prompt-based (Common App, coalition, university-specific)

### 4. Research Writing (All Grades, Emphasis in 11-12)

**Purpose:** Investigate question, synthesize multiple sources, present findings

**Research Process:**

**1. Develop Research Question:**
- Start with topic of interest
- Narrow to specific, focused question
- Example: "How does social media affect adolescent mental health?"

**2. Find and Evaluate Sources:**
- Use databases, library resources, credible websites
- Evaluate credibility:
  - **Authority:** Who wrote it? Credentials? Expertise?
  - **Accuracy:** Supported by evidence? Peer-reviewed? Citations?
  - **Currency:** When was it published? Is it current?
  - **Purpose:** Inform, persuade, sell, entertain? Bias?
  - **Coverage:** Depth of information? Comprehensive or superficial?
- Avoid unreliable sources (Wikipedia as starting point only, random blogs, biased sites)

**3. Take Notes and Organize:**
- Annotate sources
- Take notes with citation information
- Organize notes by subtopics or themes
- Avoid plagiarism: Quote, paraphrase, or summarize with credit

**4. Develop Thesis:**
- Based on research, what's your argument or main finding?
- Thesis guides organization of paper

**5. Outline and Draft:**
- Organize ideas logically
- Use sources to support claims
- Synthesize (combine multiple sources, don't just list)

**6. Cite Sources:**
- In-text citations (MLA, APA, Chicago - MLA most common in HS)
- Works Cited page (MLA) or References (APA)
- Avoid plagiarism (cite ALL sources, even if paraphrased)

---

**MLA Format (Most Common in HS):**

**In-Text Citation:**
- (Author's Last Name Page#)
- Example: (Smith 42)
- If no author: (Title Page#)

**Works Cited:**
- Alphabetical by author's last name
- Hanging indent
- Examples:
  - Book: Author. *Title*. Publisher, Year.
  - Article: Author. "Article Title." *Journal/Magazine*, vol., no., Year, pages.
  - Website: Author. "Page Title." *Website Name*, Publisher, Date, URL.

**MLA 9th Edition (current):**
- Core elements: Author. Title. Container, Contributors, Version, Number, Publisher, Date, Location.

---

**Synthesis:**
- Combine information from multiple sources
- Show relationships between sources (agree, disagree, build on each other)
- Don't just summarize each source separately
- Use sources to support YOUR argument/thesis

**Example of Synthesis:**
> Research shows conflicting views on social media's impact. Smith argues that social media increases anxiety among teens (42), while Jones contends that the effect depends on how social media is used (15). However, both agree that excessive screen time correlates with poor sleep quality (Smith 45; Jones 18).

### 5. Rhetorical Analysis (Grades 11-12, AP Lang)

**Purpose:** Analyze how author uses rhetorical strategies to achieve purpose

**SOAPS Tone:**
- **Speaker:** Who is the author/speaker? Credentials? Perspective?
- **Occasion:** What prompted this text? Context? Timing?
- **Audience:** Who is intended audience? What do they value/believe?
- **Purpose:** What does author want audience to think, feel, or do?
- **Subject:** What is the topic/issue?
- **Tone:** Author's attitude toward subject? (formal, informal, sarcastic, earnest, etc.)

**Rhetorical Strategies to Analyze:**
- Rhetorical appeals (logos, ethos, pathos)
- Diction (word choice, connotation, loaded language)
- Syntax (sentence structure, length, parallelism, repetition)
- Imagery and figurative language
- Tone and mood
- Structure and organization
- Examples, anecdotes, analogies
- Rhetorical questions, direct address

**Rhetorical Analysis Essay Structure:**
- Introduction: Introduce text, author, context, thesis about rhetorical strategies
- Body paragraphs: Analyze specific strategies and their effects
- Conclusion: Overall effectiveness of rhetoric

**Example Thesis:**
> In his "Letter from Birmingham Jail," Martin Luther King Jr. uses appeals to religious authority, vivid imagery of injustice, and parallel sentence structure to persuade white moderates that immediate action against segregation is a moral imperative.

**Real-World Applications:**
- Analyze political speeches, advertisements, opinion pieces
- Media literacy (understand how you're being persuaded)
- College composition courses
- Critical thinking about rhetoric in daily life

## The Writing Process

### 1. Prewriting (Planning)

**Brainstorming:**
- Freewriting, listing, clustering, mind mapping
- Explore ideas without judgment
- Discover what you want to say

**Researching:**
- Gather information (for informative, research, argument)
- Take notes, cite sources

**Outlining:**
- Organize ideas logically
- Formal outline (I, A, 1, a) or informal (bullet points)
- Flexible (can change during drafting)

---

### 2. Drafting

**Focus on Getting Ideas Down:**
- Don't worry about perfection
- Follow outline but be flexible
- Write body paragraphs first, introduction/conclusion later (optional)

**Thesis/Claim:**
- Clear, specific, arguable (for argument)
- States main idea or controlling idea

**Body Paragraphs:**
- Topic sentence (main idea of paragraph)
- Evidence and support
- Analysis/explanation (connect evidence to claim)
- Transitions between paragraphs

**Introduction:**
- Hook (engage reader: question, quote, anecdote, surprising fact)
- Context/background
- Thesis/claim (usually last sentence of introduction)

**Conclusion:**
- Restate thesis (in new words)
- Synthesize key points
- Broader implications, call to action, or final insight
- Avoid introducing new information

---

### 3. Revising (Improving Content and Organization)

**Big-Picture Revision:**
- **Ideas:** Is thesis clear? Is argument strong? Is evidence sufficient?
- **Organization:** Is structure logical? Are paragraphs in best order? Do transitions work?
- **Audience and Purpose:** Does writing suit audience and achieve purpose?
- **Voice and Style:** Is voice appropriate? Is writing engaging?

**Peer Review:**
- Exchange drafts with classmate
- Provide constructive feedback (strengths and suggestions)
- Ask questions, make suggestions

**Teacher Conferencing:**
- Discuss draft with teacher
- Identify strengths and areas for improvement
- Set goals for revision

---

### 4. Editing (Improving Sentences and Words)

**Sentence-Level Editing:**
- Sentence variety (vary length and structure)
- Eliminate wordiness
- Use active voice (generally)
- Parallel structure
- Clear pronoun reference

**Word Choice:**
- Precise, vivid language
- Avoid clichÃ©s, jargon, slang (in formal writing)
- Domain-specific vocabulary (where appropriate)
- Connotation (consider emotional associations)

---

### 5. Proofreading (Correcting Errors)

**Grammar and Mechanics:**
- Subject-verb agreement
- Pronoun-antecedent agreement
- Verb tense consistency
- Correct punctuation (commas, semicolons, apostrophes, etc.)
- Spelling

**Proofreading Strategies:**
- Read aloud (catch errors you miss when reading silently)
- Read backwards (focus on individual words)
- Use spell-check (but don't rely on it completely)
- Print and proofread on paper

---

### 6. Publishing/Sharing

**Final Draft:**
- Type, format correctly (MLA, APA, etc.)
- Proofread one final time
- Submit or share

**Multimodal Publishing:**
- Blog, website, digital portfolio
- Presentation, video, podcast
- Class anthology, literary magazine

## Style, Voice, and Sentence Craft

### Developing Style

**Sentence Variety:**
- Vary sentence length (short for emphasis, longer for detail and flow)
- Vary sentence structure (simple, compound, complex, compound-complex)
- Vary sentence beginnings (avoid starting every sentence the same way)

**Active vs. Passive Voice:**
- Active voice (subject performs action): "The committee approved the proposal."
- Passive voice (subject receives action): "The proposal was approved by the committee."
- Generally prefer active voice (clearer, more direct)
- Use passive when actor unknown or unimportant

**Parallelism:**
- Use same grammatical structure for items in list or series
- Example: "I like reading, writing, and running." (all gerunds)

**Conciseness:**
- Eliminate unnecessary words
- Replace wordy phrases with concise alternatives
- Example: "Due to the fact that" â†’ "Because"

---

### Voice and Tone

**Voice:**
- Writer's unique style and personality in writing
- Developed through word choice, sentence structure, humor, perspective

**Tone:**
- Writer's attitude toward subject and audience
- Formal, informal, serious, humorous, sarcastic, earnest, etc.
- Match tone to audience and purpose

**Formal vs. Informal:**
- **Formal:** Academic writing, professional writing (avoid contractions, slang, first person)
- **Informal:** Personal narrative, creative writing, blog (can use contractions, casual language, first person)

## Grammar and Conventions (High School Expectations)

### Common Grammar Issues to Master

**Subject-Verb Agreement:**
- Singular subject = singular verb
- Plural subject = plural verb
- Watch for tricky cases (indefinite pronouns, collective nouns, inverted sentences)

**Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement:**
- Pronoun must agree with noun it replaces (number, gender)
- Avoid vague pronoun reference (unclear what pronoun refers to)

**Pronoun Case:**
- Subject pronouns (I, he, she, we, they)
- Object pronouns (me, him, her, us, them)
- Possessive pronouns (my, his, her, our, their, its - no apostrophe!)

**Verb Tense:**
- Maintain consistent verb tense
- Shift only when time changes

**Comma Rules:**
- Items in a series (Oxford comma recommended)
- After introductory elements
- Between independent clauses joined by coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS)
- Around nonessential clauses/phrases
- NOT between subject and verb, or verb and object

**Semicolons:**
- Between independent clauses without conjunction
- Between items in complex series

**Apostrophes:**
- Possessive (John's book, students' work)
- Contractions (don't, it's = it is)
- NOT for plurals!

**Sentence Fragments and Run-Ons:**
- Fragment: Missing subject or verb, or doesn't express complete thought
- Run-on: Two independent clauses incorrectly joined
- Fix run-ons: Period, semicolon, comma + conjunction, or subordination

**Modifiers:**
- Place modifiers next to words they modify
- Avoid dangling modifiers
