# NYC Multilingual Learner (MLL) Framework
**District:** New York City Department of Education
**Students:** 150,000+ MLLs (14% of enrollment)
**Languages:** 180+ languages spoken
**Assessment:** NYSESLAT (NYS English as a Second Language Achievement Test)

---

## Overview

NYC DOE serves over **150,000 Multilingual Learners** (MLLs), formerly called English Language Learners (ELLs). Students speak **180+ home languages**, with the top languages being Spanish, Chinese (Mandarin and Cantonese), Arabic, Bengali, and Russian.

**Core Principle:** Home languages are assets, not deficits. Materials must support MLLs while honoring and leveraging their linguistic and cultural backgrounds.

---

## Terminology

### NYC DOE Uses "Multilingual Learner" (MLL):

**Preferred Term:** Multilingual Learner (MLL)
**Replaces:** English Language Learner (ELL)
**Rationale:** Asset-based framing; recognizes students' multilingual capabilities

**In Materials:**
- Use "MLL" or "Multilingual Learner" (not ELL)
- Refer to "home language" (not "native language" or "first language")
- Emphasize bilingualism/multilingualism as strength

---

## NYC MLL Proficiency Levels

### 5-Level System (Based on NYSESLAT):

NYC uses the **New York State English as a Second Language Achievement Test (NYSESLAT)** which has 5 proficiency levels:

**Level 1: Entering**
- Minimal English proficiency
- Relies heavily on home language
- Single words and short phrases
- Visual supports essential

**Level 2: Emerging**
- Limited English proficiency
- Short sentences with errors
- Vocabulary developing
- Needs substantial scaffolding

**Level 3: Transitioning**
- Intermediate English proficiency
- Longer sentences with some errors
- Grade-level content with supports
- Increasing independence

**Level 4: Expanding**
- Advanced English proficiency (approaching grade-level)
- Complex sentences with minor errors
- Academic language developing
- Minimal scaffolding needed

**Level 5: Commanding**
- Proficient in English (near/at grade-level)
- Complex language structures
- Academic language proficient
- Ready for mainstream instruction (may still benefit from supports)

---

## NYSESLAT Assessment

### What It Measures:

**Four Modalities:**
- Listening
- Speaking
- Reading
- Writing

**Proficiency Determination:**
- Annual assessment (spring)
- Composite score determines proficiency level (1-5)
- Students exit MLL status at Commanding (Level 5) with certain score thresholds
- Former MLLs monitored for 2 years post-exit

## Scaffolding by Proficiency Level

### Materials must provide differentiated supports for all 5 levels:

---

### Level 1: Entering

**Language Characteristics:**
- Understands very little academic English
- Uses single words or short phrases
- Heavy reliance on visuals and gestures
- May code-switch to home language frequently

**Required Scaffolds:**

**Visuals:**
- Labeled images and diagrams
- Icons and symbols for key concepts
- Photo/video supports
- Graphic organizers with pictures

**Linguistic:**
- Bilingual glossaries (Spanish, Chinese, Arabic, Bengali, Russian at minimum)
- Sentence frames: "The _____ is _____."
- Word banks with images
- Translation tools available

**Instructional:**
- Partner with Level 3-5 peer (same home language if possible)
- Pre-teach vocabulary with visuals
- Hands-on activities
- Extended time for all tasks
- Bilingual support (paraprofessional, peer, teacher)

**Assessment:**
- Demonstrate understanding non-verbally (pointing, drawing, matching)
- Word-level responses acceptable
- Native language responses allowed where appropriate

---

### Level 2: Emerging

**Language Characteristics:**
- Understands simple academic English
- Produces short sentences (subject-verb-object)
- Developing basic vocabulary
- Grammatical errors common

**Required Scaffolds:**

**Visuals:**
- Charts and diagrams
- Timelines and flowcharts
- Visual vocabulary cards
- Annotated images

**Linguistic:**
- Sentence frames: "I think _____ because _____."
- Phrase banks for common academic tasks
- Simplified texts (same content, reduced complexity)
- Bilingual glossaries

**Instructional:**
- Collaborative learning (mixed proficiency groups)
- Vocabulary pre-teaching with examples
- Modeling and think-alouds
- Chunked texts with guiding questions
- Extended time

**Assessment:**
- Short constructed responses acceptable
- Sentence-level responses
- Focus on content understanding (not grammar perfection)
- Multiple-choice and matching to demonstrate comprehension

---

### Level 3: Transitioning

**Language Characteristics:**
- Understands grade-level content with supports
- Produces sentences and short paragraphs
- Academic vocabulary developing
- Some grammatical errors

**Required Scaffolds:**

**Visuals:**
- Graphic organizers
- Concept maps
- Data visualizations
- Strategic diagrams

**Linguistic:**
- Sentence starters: "Based on the evidence, I conclude..."
- Academic vocabulary lists with student-friendly definitions
- Text annotation guides
- Language frames for complex tasks

**Instructional:**
- Reciprocal teaching
- Think-Pair-Share protocols
- Close reading with sentence-level scaffolds
- Strategic grouping (homogeneous and heterogeneous)
- Some extended time

**Assessment:**
- Paragraph-level responses
- Emerging use of academic language
- Content mastery demonstrated with linguistic supports
- Rubrics account for language development

---

### Level 4: Expanding

**Language Characteristics:**
- Approaching grade-level English proficiency
- Produces multi-paragraph responses
- Academic vocabulary strong
- Minor grammatical errors

**Required Scaffolds:**

**Visuals:**
- Complex diagrams and charts (as for all students)
- Organizational tools

**Linguistic:**
- Sentence starters for sophisticated arguments
- Domain-specific vocabulary support
- Transition word banks
- Models of exemplar writing

**Instructional:**
- Academic discussions with protocols
- Independent reading with check-ins
- Peer collaboration
- Occasional pre-teaching of complex vocabulary
- Minimal extended time

**Assessment:**
- Multi-paragraph responses expected
- Academic language use assessed
- Near grade-level expectations
- Minor language errors acceptable

---

### Level 5: Commanding

**Language Characteristics:**
- Grade-level or above English proficiency
- Sophisticated language structures
- Academic language proficient
- Minimal errors

**Required Scaffolds:**

**Visuals:**
- Standard grade-level visuals

**Linguistic:**
- Advanced vocabulary enrichment
- Idiomatic expression support (as needed)
- Register and style guidance (formal vs. informal)

**Instructional:**
- Full grade-level instruction
- Enrichment and extension
- Leadership roles in collaborative work
- Monitor for continued progress

**Assessment:**
- Full grade-level expectations
- May still benefit from accommodations (extended time in some cases)
- Monitored for 2 years post-exit from MLL status

## Cross-Proficiency Best Practices

### Applicable to ALL MLL levels:

**1. Home Language as Asset:**
- Encourage use of home language for thinking and discussion
- Provide bilingual materials where possible
- Value translanguaging (using multiple languages fluidly)
- Connect to cultural background knowledge

**2. Collaborative Learning:**
- Strategic grouping (mixed and same proficiency)
- Peer support and mentoring
- Academic conversations with protocols
- Structured discussion formats

**3. Multimodal Expression:**
- Allow multiple ways to demonstrate understanding
- Visual, oral, written, kinesthetic options
- Technology tools for creation and communication
- Choice in assessment format (where appropriate)

**4. Explicit Language Instruction:**
- Pre-teach academic vocabulary
- Model academic language use
- Practice with sentence frames and starters
- Feedback on language development (not just content)

**5. Cultural Responsiveness:**
- Diverse representation in materials
- Culturally relevant contexts and examples
- Respect for diverse backgrounds
- Inclusive classroom environment

## NYC-Specific MLL Programs

### Program Types:

**1. English as a New Language (ENL):**
- Dedicated ENL instruction (stand-alone or integrated)
- Certified ENL teachers
- Focus on English language development across modalities

**2. Bilingual Education:**
- Content instruction in home language and English
- Transitional (grades K-6) or Dual Language (K-12)
- Available in Spanish, Chinese, Haitian Creole, Arabic, Bengali, Russian, etc.

**3. Dual Language Programs:**
- 50/50 or 90/10 models
- MLLs and native English speakers together
- Goal: Bilingualism and biliteracy for all

**Materials Implication:**
Materials should support all program models with appropriate scaffolds and, ideally, home language resources.

## Integration with Other Frameworks

### MLL Supports + MLRs (Math):

**Math Language Routines are designed for MLLs:**
- MLR1: Stronger and Clearer Each Time (language practice)
- MLR2: Collect and Display (capture student language)
- MLR8: Discussion Supports (sentence frames, revoicing)

**Application:** Use MLRs with proficiency-level differentiation

**Example:**
- **Level 1-2:** MLR8 with simple sentence frames ("I agree with ___ because ___")
- **Level 3-4:** MLR1 with academic language refinement
- **Level 5:** MLRs for enrichment and precision

---

### MLL Supports + UDL:

**Universal Design for Learning benefits MLLs:**
- Multiple means of representation (visuals, audio, text)
- Multiple means of action/expression (choice in how to demonstrate learning)
- Multiple means of engagement (culturally relevant contexts)

**Application:** UDL + MLL scaffolds create access for all

---

### MLL Supports + CR-SE:

**Culturally Responsive-Sustaining Education for MLLs:**
- Honor home languages and cultures
- Connect to cultural background knowledge
- Diverse representation in materials
- Asset-based view of multilingualism

**Application:** Every MLL scaffold should be culturally responsive
