# High School Government Guide
**Course Level:** Typically Grade 12 (US Government), Grade 11-12 (AP US Government and Politics)
**Applies To:** All high school Government and AP US Government and Politics courses

---

## Overview

US Government examines American political institutions, processes, and civic participation. Students analyze the Constitution, branches of government, civil liberties and rights, political participation, and contemporary policy debates.

**College/Career Readiness:** Government prepares students for informed citizenship and careers in law, politics, public policy, journalism, and public service.

**State Requirements:** Government/Civics is required for graduation in most states.

**AP US Government and Politics:** College-level course emphasizing political concepts, institutions, and policy-making processes.

---

## Key Topics

### 1. Constitutional Foundations

- Articles of Confederation
- Constitutional Convention (compromises, debates)
- Federalism (division of powers between national and state)
- Separation of powers and checks and balances
- Bill of Rights and subsequent amendments
- Federalist Papers

### 2. Legislative Branch (Congress)

- Structure (House of Representatives, Senate)
- Powers (enumerated, implied, inherent)
- Legislative process (how a bill becomes law)
- Committees, leadership
- Representation and constituencies

### 3. Executive Branch (President)

- Powers and roles (commander-in-chief, chief diplomat, chief executive, etc.)
- Executive Office of the President
- Cabinet and bureaucracy
- Foreign policy

### 4. Judicial Branch (Supreme Court)

- Structure (federal court system)
- Judicial review (*Marbury v. Madison*)
- Landmark Supreme Court cases
- Interpretation (strict constructionism vs. judicial activism)

### 5. Civil Liberties (Bill of Rights)

- 1st Amendment: speech, religion, press, assembly, petition
- 2nd Amendment: right to bear arms
- 4th Amendment: search and seizure
- 5th/6th Amendments: due process, right to counsel
- 8th Amendment: cruel and unusual punishment
- 14th Amendment: incorporation, equal protection

### 6. Civil Rights

- Civil Rights Movement
- Equality: race, gender, disability, LGBTQ+
- Voting rights
- Affirmative action
- Landmark legislation: Civil Rights Acts, Voting Rights Act, ADA

### 7. Political Participation

- Voting (turnout, barriers, reforms)
- Political parties (history, structure, functions)
- Elections (primaries, general, Electoral College)
- Interest groups and lobbying
- Media and public opinion
- Campaigns and political socialization

### 8. Public Policy

- Domestic policy (healthcare, education, environment, economy)
- Foreign policy (diplomacy, defense, alliances)
- Policy-making process (agenda-setting, formulation, adoption, implementation, evaluation)

## AP US Government and Politics Format

**Multiple Choice:** Interpret data, text, visuals related to government concepts
**Free Response:**
- Concept Application (analyze scenario)
- Quantitative Analysis (analyze data, charts)
- SCOTUS Comparison (compare required case to non-required case)
- Argument Essay (develop argument on political topic)

## Quick Reference

**Every Government Material Must:**
- Cover Constitution and all three branches
- Teach civil liberties and civil rights
- Develop informed citizenship
- (AP) Align to AP US Gov CED, include required SCOTUS cases
- Encourage civic participation and engagement
- Analyze contemporary policy issues

**Remember:** Government education is essential for democracy. Informed, engaged citizens are the foundation of self-governance.
